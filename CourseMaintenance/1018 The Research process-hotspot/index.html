Develop the research question, aims, and hypotheses

Research questions, foundational to any study, stem from two primary sources: real-world observations and building upon existing research. For instance, observing that individuals who take short naps have better memory recall can lead to a question like: "Does taking a nap improve memory consolidation?" Meanwhile, existing research can be a source of inspiration, where examining prior studies helps in identifying gaps, refining the research question, and contributing new insights.

Once the research question is identified, the study's aims are established, which might include investigating the impact of napping on memory consolidation in adults. Following this, a hypothesis is formulated, such as: "Adults who take a short nap after learning new information will demonstrate better memory consolidation compared to those who do not nap." Crucially, this hypothesis presents a testable statement about the expected relationship between the variables and serves as a guiding principle for the data collection and analysis process, ensuring the study's focus and direction.

Research design and proposal

Once aims or hypotheses are defined, researchers develop a research proposal. This stage involves planning the research timeline, determining the sample(s) to be studied, selecting appropriate research methods, and choosing an analysis approach. Each aspect of the research design is carefully considered to ensure the study's integrity and validity.

Seek Funding Opportunities

Securing funding is essential for conducting research. Researchers actively search for funding opportunities from various sources, such as research grants provided by funding agencies or institutions. Adequate financial support enables researchers to execute their studies effectively. An example of a government-based funding body is the Australian Research Council.

Submit Proposal to Ethics Committee

Ethical considerations play a critical role in research involving human participants. The ethics committee carefully examines the research proposal to assess its ethical considerations. This includes reviewing the potential risks and benefits to participants, ensuring informed consent procedures are appropriate, and considering issues such as privacy, confidentiality, and potential conflicts of interest.

In Australia, research involving human participants is governed by the National Statement on Ethical Conduct in Human Research, which outlines the principles and guidelines for conducting ethical research.

Implement the Research Proposal

With ethical approval obtained, researchers proceed to implement their research proposals. This stage involves recruiting participants and/or collecting data using the chosen research methods. Researchers meticulously execute their plans to gather reliable and valid data.

Analyse Data

Once the data is collected, researchers proceed to analyse it. The following example demonstrates the three broad phases of data analysis. Example: A study examining the effects of exercise on mood in university students.

Example: A study examining the effects of exercise on mood in university students.

  • Data Preparation: Researchers gather data on university students' exercise habits and mood levels, organise the information, correct mistakes, and ensure consistency. They may create variables for exercise frequency and mood ratings.
  • Descriptive Statistics: Researchers calculate basic statistics like average exercise frequency and mood scores, and may visualise the distribution of these variables among participants using graphs or charts.
  • Inferential Statistics: Researchers analyse data to assess if exercise frequency relates to mood changes. They compare the mood of students who exercise regularly with those who don't, seeking differences indicating exercise's impact on mood.

You will be exploring a lot more about this aspect of the research process throughout this course!

Finalise Findings

After analysing the data, researchers synthesise their findings and draw conclusions. They interpret the results in relation to their research aims or hypotheses, making connections to existing theories or previous research. This stage is crucial in shaping the overall narrative of the study. Document Research Process and Findings

Researchers meticulously document their research process, findings, and interpretations in a manuscript. This comprehensive record allows others to understand and replicate the research. Researchers ensure clarity and adhere to academic writing standards in their documentation. You will be asked to learn how to apply APA 7 formatting to almost every assignment from now on (and when you write your own journal articles in the future!). So, take a moment to familiarise yourself with this important resource from the American Psychological Assocation on APA style: APA Style Blog (you can use the search function at the top of the page to look up how to cite a book, for example).

Submit Manuscript to Peer-Reviewed Journal

To share their work with the scientific community, researchers submit their manuscripts to peer-reviewed journals. The manuscript undergoes a rigorous evaluation process conducted by experts in the field. These reviewers assess the quality, validity, and contribution of the research. Watch the following video to understand the peer-review process in more detail: Peer Review in 3 Minutes

Make Revisions and Publication

Based on the feedback received from reviewers, researchers may need to revise their manuscript. The revision process ensures that any concerns or suggestions raised by the reviewers are adequately addressed. If no revisions are required, the manuscript is published, making the research findings accessible to a wider audience. Then, you will be able to find them in databases like PsycInfo (a database for psychology research – save this as a favourite on your toolbar!)

Consider Research Extension

After completing a study, researchers reflect on the potential for further investigation or extension. If there are opportunities to build upon the existing research, researchers can initiate a new research cycle by developing new aims or hypotheses. This iterative process contributes to the continuous expansion of knowledge.
